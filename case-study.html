Redesigning Onboarding for a FinTech App

Overview

Pave is a personal finance app helping young professionals track spending, set savings goals, and understand their money. Despite strong product-market fit signals, the app was hemorrhaging users in the first five minutes — before they ever saw the dashboard.

I was brought in as the lead designer to audit the onboarding flow, run qualitative and quantitative research, and ship a complete redesign. The constraint: no new engineering resources, same backend APIs, four-month timeline.

"42% of users abandoned the app during the 7-step account creation flow. Most never returned."

The Problem

The original onboarding asked users to connect their bank account, verify identity, set financial goals, and configure notifications — all before showing a single piece of value. It was a consent parade in a trust desert.

Three core failure modes emerged from the initial analysis:

  • Premature commitment — Users were asked for sensitive data (SSN, bank login) within the first two screens.
  • Cognitive overload — 11 form fields crammed into a single "goals" screen.
  • No perceived progress — No indication of how many steps remained; users felt stuck in a void.

Research

I ran three research sprints over five weeks. The goal was to understand not just where users dropped off, but why — the emotional state they were in and the mental model they brought.

Moderated usability sessions

12 participants, recruited via Maze panel to match Pave's target demographic (ages 23–34, first-time investors). Each session was 45 minutes. I asked users to think aloud as they moved through the existing flow. The same phrases kept surfacing: "Why do they need that?", "How much longer is this?", "This feels sketchy."

Funnel analytics audit

Working with the data team, I mapped Mixpanel events to specific UI moments. The steepest cliff — 31% single-step drop — was at the bank connection screen. A secondary cliff appeared at the goals configuration step.

Competitive teardown

I analyzed onboarding flows for 14 competitors and adjacent apps (Robinhood, Monzo, Revolut, Chime). The pattern was clear: apps that deferred identity verification until after the first "aha moment" saw 2–4× better activation rates.

Design Process

I structured the redesign around a single strategic principle: earn trust before asking for it. Every screen needed to either deliver value or build confidence — ideally both.

Information architecture

I mapped the existing 11-step flow and identified which steps could be deferred until after activation, parallelized, or eliminated entirely. The new flow reduced mandatory pre-activation steps from 11 to 4.

I ran two rounds of design sprints with the PM and engineering lead, each producing 3–4 high-fidelity prototypes for unmoderated Maze tests. Below are representative screens from Rounds 1 and 2.

The Solution

The final design restructured onboarding into three phases: Explore, Commit, and Optimize. Users reach the dashboard after just two screens. Everything else happens contextually, when the user has a reason to care.

Key design decisions

  1. Demo mode first — New users land in a read-only demo dashboard populated with sample data. They see the product before giving anything.
  2. Progressive permission requests — Bank connection is requested only when a user tries to add a real account, with explicit "why we need this" copy and security badges.
  3. Step indicator — A minimal progress bar with labeled steps eliminates uncertainty. Tested three variants; the labeled version outperformed the bare progress bar by 18%.
  4. Inline validation — Form fields validate as the user types, catching errors before submission and reducing frustration-induced abandonment.

Results

The redesigned flow shipped to 100% of users in Week 14. Results were measured over the following 8 weeks.

The demo mode was an unexpected hit — 38% of users who entered demo mode went on to create a full account within the same session, a conversion rate the team hadn't seen for any previous acquisition channel.

Learnings

The biggest shift in my thinking from this project: the best onboarding doesn't feel like onboarding. When users are exploring a product rather than completing a form, their defenses drop. Trust follows experience, not explanation.

I also learned to fight harder for research time early in the process. The usability sessions in Week 1 surfaced the "trust gap" framing that ultimately drove every major design decision. Without that, we'd have been optimizing copy rather than rethinking the architecture.

"The best onboarding doesn't feel like onboarding — it feels like using the product."
